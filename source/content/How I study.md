# Entering a new subject
Getting a grip of something entirely new is always difficult. Good thing is that someone had to invent/create/discover/define it. So there should always be documentation/tutorials. Use them, read them, watch them.
## Get an overview of the topics
If I really don't know where to start with a topic I either ==try to find a connection to a topic i already know== about or ==just jump into the part that seems most appealing== to me.
Like in [[AC I Overview]] i break down the subject into their topics and dive into them individually the connections between them come with effort and time. Of course breaking a syllabus into topics is usually easy, but you can find such a breakdown for anything online.
## Have a goal
In the past over-studying or studying topics outside of what is being asked on the test has been a challenge for me. Set a goal and be honest with yourself if you are working towards it. Are you just doing what *feels* productive or are you actually doing the work that **is** productive?
Watching a video on how to do differential equations can be helpful to get into the topic and learn or remember how to do it in the beginning. **That is not studying.**
And dont get me wrong that is not just *my* opinion.
##  Nothing beats the real thing 
You have to ==do the thing== you want to get better at yourself. ==Over and over again.==
Watching some else do it wont make you improve after the first time.
### Source?
I didnt make it up.
[Andrew Huberman has a whole podcast with cited reliable sources about this topic](https://www.hubermanlab.com/episode/optimal-protocols-for-studying-learning) (btw listening to this is also not studying). 
Its the [basis of spaced repetition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaced_repetition#Evidence_and_criticism) which is widely considered [better than traditional learning](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.00962/full). It [uses advanced methods](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2017.00962/full) to measure how you remember, forget and relearn subjects after dropping them for a while.
# The right tool for the job
## FSRS
 [Anki](https://apps.ankiweb.net/) is the king of spaced repetion. 
 - It is free
 	- free to use (aside from Apple devices)
 	- free to create/change/share decks
 - Its easy to use, has plenty of documentation and tutorials on how to use it
 - It syncs to other devices via ankiweb
 - Any other features you could think of are available as [free community sourced plugins](https://ankiweb.net/shared/addons)
 - Even [community shared decks](https://ankiweb.net/shared/decks)
## Note Taking

 Use whatever medium works. There are [different methods on how to structure your notes](https://oxfordlearning.com/5-effective-note-taking-methods/), but i wouldnt get to deep into it. **KISS** - **K**eep **i**t **s**imple **s**tupid
 Even with this website up, I still write on paper **a lot**. Specially when I study something more math heavy.  
## Studying with others
I generally avoid it in the beginning. To me it is detrimental to study with others when i myself haven't spent some time diving into the subject at hand. After that period (for intense uni courses that often falls after 1-2 Weeks of study) I think it becomes extremely productive to do.
Having someone else to correct, question and guide you on topics or questions that don't sit quite right yet is amazing. I'm a big fan of using a whiteboard/blackboard and taking turn in solving problems or solving them side by side and comparing afterwards. Again, [[#Nothing beats the real thing]]. Don't just sit there and talk about how to solve the problem or draw the diagram. Go up there and prove to me that you can do it, i don't believe you.
## Pomodoro
Can be good if your brain works well with it. I use it alongside ["study with me"-streams](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=study+with+me) and do 50min work 10 min break for 3-6h. My productivity plummets after 6h so I simply don't do more. Sometimes that means another couple days of study compared to my peers but honestly [I do not care](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9QA8QYOATUw).
# Sleep
Yes, sleep.
It's best to treat sleep as just as important as studying. Get the sleep your body needs (your mileage may vary 7-8h seems to be standard).
## Quality $\neq$ Quantity
Feeling rested and having slept 8h have surprisingly little to do with each other depending on your body. If you want to quality-max Andrew Huberman provides a [Toolkit for sleep](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/toolkit-for-sleep) and a [deep dive into the points discussed](https://www.hubermanprotocols.org/andrew-hubermans-sleep-protocols) for free. I think more people should take their sleep seriously but still, **KISS** - **K**eep **i**t **s**imple **s**tupid.

> [!Error] All-nighters are a lose-lose
> They don't offer you any meaningful study results and [ruin your mental and physical performance](https://www.academia.edu/31552239/The_Link_Between_Sleep_Quantity_and_Academic_Performance_for_the_College_Student) [for at least a day](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/chapter/bookseries/abs/pii/B9780444537027000075?via%3Dihub).
